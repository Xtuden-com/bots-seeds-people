Appraisal

The process of identifying materials offered to an archives that have sufficient value to be accessioned.

RQ: How is appraisal being enacted in web archives?

It seemed like an interesting question given this unique socio-technical system comprised of bots, software systems, algorithms, archivists, and organizations. And one that has a lot of CSCW shape to it.

We had started with two additional research questions but realized during the review process that we really only adequately answer this one.
  • How do technologies for web crawling and harvesting–seed lists and scopes–figure in their appraisal decisions?
  • Are there opportunities to design more useful sys- tems for the appraisal of content for Web archives?
Our most important and intellectually demanding task as archivists is to make an informed selection of information that will provide the future with a representative record of human experience in our time. But why must we do it so badly? Is there any other field of information gathering that has such a broad mandate with a selection process so random, so fragmented, so uncoordinated, and even so often accidental?

    • As we've seen the question of how people and machines work together to archive the web is certainly has a quantitative componenta to it. But our question of how people (archivists) ascribe value to web content was also fundamentally qualitative.

    We thought about doing surveys, but surveys of the web archiving community have been done, which provide general pictures of the types of organizations, tools, procedures at a high level. We weren't exactly sure what questions to ask on a survey to get at this question of appraisal. We were interested in thick descriptions of the appraisal process itself. What factors went into deciding what materials to collect? What tools did they actually use in their work? What did their daily routines look like?
